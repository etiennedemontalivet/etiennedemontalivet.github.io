Neuro Rehabilitation

  1. Home

I worked for 3 years at the Institut of Intelligent Systems and Robotics (ISIR). I developed from scratch an upperlimb prosthesis (software, electronics and hardware) to study its control by transradial amputees.

Indeed, while the hardware prosthesis improved a lot the last decade, its control remained primary: all commercial upperlimb prosthesis for transradial amputees in 2018 have a myoelectric control of the prosthesis that uses muscles contractions from biceps/triceps. It is the same as controling the prosthesis with 2 ON/OFF buttons.

To improve the prosthesis control of transradial amputees, I worked with Dr NathanaÃ«l Jarrasse on multiple approaches that uses either phantom limb movements or existing synergy in our daily movements.

Phantom Limb control

One way of controlling the prosthesis was through the phantom limb movements. Some amputees still have residual movement possibilities of their amputed limb. We recorded them with EMGs, labelled them and use a machine learning algorithm to classify the different movements. We then program the prosthesis to mimic the detected movement.

Synergy based elbow control

This approach uses the synergy existing in a movement. The assumption is that the elbow opening is directly function of the residual limb movement. We recorded multiple tasks with IMUs, created a model that gives the elbow opening angle given the IMUs quaternions + accelerations and compared it with common myoelectric control.

Human-in-the-loop Machine Learning

The prosthesis control case is very interesting in a machine learning point of view because we train a model, often based on a baseline experiment involving people, then we ask them to reproduce the task (a movement), hoping that the model will classify it. But, sometimes because of some setup limitations (time to classify > ~200 ms), sometimes because of the task itself, people change their behaviour using the model.

This change could be a weakness because the model did not see these data, so it probably will lead to wrong predictions. But it could also be a strenght: in the phantom limb based control approach, we use this capacity from human to learn, to adapt to guide them to a movement that itself is "better understanding" from the algorithm (the corresponding class is further from the others). I wrote a scientific paper on this. We showed that we were able to retrain a specific movement class, highly improving its False and True Positive rates while keeping the other movements/classes at the same rates.

The above figure shows in A. the confusion matrices before and after the retraining session. We see the the retrained class (red rectangle) goes from 12% of right detection to 67%. This evolution is represented in B. with red/green color. In C., we see that the patient has changed its movement strategy, which leads to a global accuracy improvement in B.
